# Red Flags in Social Engineering

This file is my working guide to common warning signs used in phishing and vishing attacks.

The goal is to help me recognise patterns, not just react emotionally or too quickly to each message or phone call.

## 1. Urgency
Urgency tries to make the target act quickly before they stop and think.

Examples:
- “Act now”
- “Final warning”
- “Your account will be locked today”

Why this matters:
People are more likely to make mistakes when they feel rushed.

## 2. Authority
Authority is when the attacker pretends to be someone trusted or important.

Examples:
- bank representative
- IT support
- manager
- telecom provider

Why this matters:
People may lower their guard when they believe the request is official.

## 3. Fear
Fear is used to create panic and push the target into acting quickly.

Examples:
- suspicious transaction alert
- account suspension warning
- payment failure threat
- security breach message

Why this matters:
Fear can make people react before they verify the message or call.

## 4. Emotional Manipulation
The attacker uses emotion such as sympathy, guilt, panic, or distress.

Examples:
- crying child in the background
- “your relative needs help”
- “you must act right now”
- “this is your last chance”

Why this matters:
Strong emotion can stop people from thinking clearly.

## 5. Credential or MFA Request
The message or caller asks for sensitive login details or one-time codes.

Examples:
- password reset code
- one-time code
- MFA approval
- login verification code

Why this matters:
Real organisations should not ask for passwords or one-time codes through suspicious channels.

## 6. Suspicious Link or Callback Path
The target is pushed toward an untrusted link, phone number, QR code, or attachment.

Examples:
- strange URL
- shortened link
- unknown attachment
- unofficial callback number
- unexpected QR code

Why this matters:
Attackers often try to control the path the victim takes.

## 7. Identity Mismatch
The claimed identity does not match the contact details, tone, or context.

Examples:
- display name does not match email domain
- unusual writing style from a known contact
- caller claims one company but asks unrelated questions

Why this matters:
Small inconsistencies are often early warning signs.

## 8. Payment or Account Change Request
The message pushes for money movement or a sensitive account update.

Examples:
- gift card request
- bank detail change
- invoice update
- SIM/account recovery request

Why this matters:
These actions can cause financial loss, account takeover or unauthorised access.

## 9. Secrecy or Isolation
The attacker tries to stop the victim from checking with someone else.

Examples:
- “do not tell anyone”
- “stay on the line”
- “don’t hang up”
- “this is confidential”

Why this matters:
Verification is one of the best defenses, so attackers try to block it.

## 10. Unusual Context
The request does not fit the normal process or usual communication style.

Examples:
- request sent outside normal hours
- unusual communication channel
- no ticket, no approval, no normal process
- message feels out of character

Why this matters:
Even when the wording looks polished, the overall context may still be wrong.

## Quick Questions I Should Ask
When reviewing a suspicious message or phone call, I should ask:

1. What is the attacker trying to get?
2. Why would a normal person trust this?
3. What are the warning signs?
4. What should the safe response be?

## Simple Safe Responses
Useful defensive actions include:
- pause and do not act immediately
- do not click the link
- do not share passwords or one-time codes
- verify using an official website, app, or known phone number
- report the message or call through the correct channel
